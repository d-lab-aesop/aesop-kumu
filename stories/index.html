Working with stories

Why stories?​

For as long as humans have had language, we have relied on stories to make sense of the world.

We tell ourselves stories in order to understand how the world around us works. One way we process our day to day experiences is through constructing narratives of cause and effect. By deciphering why things happened a certain way, and incorporating this new knowledge into our mental models of the universe, we teach ourselves how to act in similar situations in the future.

We tell each other stories for all kinds of reasons: to share, to teach, to entertain, to deceive, to bond. These stories are a portal into our inner lives. They contain not only an account of our experiences, but also hints about our dreams, values, beliefs, and motivations.

At the D-Lab, we believe that by building a map of tangible and intangible factors as told by many individual storytellers, we may be able to locate the key to understanding collective human behavior.

Anatomy of a story pt. I​

For the purposes of AESOP, a story consists of a description of events and states, connected by cause and effect. At minimum, a story must contain the answers to two questions:

  1. "What happened?"
  2. "Why?"

What: Events and States​

We consider an event to be something that happens, whereas a state is a condition that is capable of changing or varying over a measurable period of time.

The two concepts are intrinsically linked: events lead to changes in state, and states change from one to another by way of events. Both address the what question.

This is not a story:

Example 1. The chicken crossed the road.

Rather than a story, example 1 is simply a description of an event. Alternatively, we could interpret it as a change from one state (the chicken is on side A of the road) to another (the chicken is on side B of the road). At any rate, we are able to derive no further information from the text; we have what, but not why.

Why: Cause and Effect​

This is a story:

Example 2. The chicken crossed the road to avoid a cat that was on the same side.

Here, the conjunction to clearly indicates a relationship between a cause (state: the presence of a predator) and an effect (event: crossing the road). In reading it, we get a sense of both the what and the why.

The signifiers of causality are not always as obvious. For example, these are also valid stories:

Example 3. Noticing a cat nearby, the chicken crossed the road.

Example 4. A chicken and a cat were on the same side of the road. The chicken crossed the road.

While example 3 attributes the road-crossing event to the same cause as example 2, the narrator does so without using explicitly causal language (e.g. to, so, because, consequently, as a result). The reader of the story must infer the link through a combination of syntax and general knowledge about chickens and cats.

Example 4 requires the reader to make an even greater leap. We are not told that the chicken is even aware of the cat's presence; we are simply given the contextual information that the two animals were at one point on the same side of the road, and left to infer the rest.

We will return to this later, but for now please remember that:

  1. An AESOP story must contain cause and effect.
  2. Contextual information can hold implicit causal relationships.

From sources to stories​

The first step in an AESOP analysis is to standardize, simplify, and separate our source material. We do this for two reasons:

  1. A source (e.g. an interview transcript) might need to be edited for clarity, conciseness, or privacy.
  2. Breaking up lengthy texts makes it easier to trace higher-level insights back to their original source.

We'll use a short excerpt from Alice in Wonderland as a stand-in for an actual interview transcript.

File duplication and tracking changes​

Create a duplicate of the file containing your raw data, and work exclusively in the duplicate file with change-tracking turned on. Do not modify your raw data; make every effort to maintain an unbroken chain of evidence from raw data to conclusions drawn.

The workflow shown in these screenshots is based on Microsoft Word, but similar functionality exists in Google Docs, Pages, and other word processing applications.

Anonymizing​

Protect your subjects' privacy by redacting any sensitive or identifying information in accordance with your human subjects study protocol. Here, the name Alice is being replaced with the subject code subject_01

Editing stories​

Strive for a balance between clarity and detail in editing. On the one hand, you should simplify and paraphrase where necessary to protect the privacy and clarify the intent of the interview subjects; on the other hand, you should be wary of over-editing, since contextual details that strike you as superfluous might spark an insight from another researcher coming from a different perspective.

Try to preserve the authentic voice of the speaker as much as possible—you may want to justify an insight later on by presenting it in a subject's own words.

Since stories might later be read outside of the context of the present interview or study, be sure to add in any contextual particulars that might help a newcomer make sense of it. Define uncommon terms and abbreviations at least once per story using parentheticals. If you collected your data using structured or semi-structured interviews, you might want to incorporate parts of the interview questions/prompts into the story as supporting context.

Focus on removing unnecessary text while preserving the sense of the story. The five "W" questions are a useful tool for thinking about how to do this:

“Who does what, when, where, and why?”

Splitting into scenes​

Once you feel that you've simplified the text sufficiently, use numbered headings to subdivide the story into individual scenes.

You can think of scenes as mini-stories. Each scene should meet the what and why requirements of an AESOP story, and each one should be under 200 words—ideally closer to 100. There are a couple of advantages to subdividing stories into scenes: it allows us to refer more precisely to specific parts of a story, and shorter chunks of text are easier to view in a variety of computer programs.

Save the raw and edited files in a secure location, and continue to the next section for instructions on how to create a database and upload your data to it.
